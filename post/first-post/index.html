Post #1: Looking back at Ludum Dare 42

Nov 28, 2018

First post. Let’s take a look at Ludum Dare 42.

Let’s start this devblog with something interesting.

I don’t expect anyone to read this blog, since I’m not really sharing this anywhere yet. I’m mainly making this so I can hold myself accountable for personal projects, since I want to really get down and work on something big.

With that brief introduction out of the way, let’s start by talking about Ludum Dare. The 43th Ludum Dare jam is happening this weekend, so I may as well make my first post about that.

Post #1: Looking back at LD42

Ludum Dare.

48 hours, one game. It’s a test of strength, willpower, and the ability to make the most out of one’s tools.

I’ve tried participating in it a couple of times, and only managed to complete a compo game twice, the last of which was made over the summer.

LD 42

For the last Jam, I made a game called Astropocalypse for the theme “Running out of Space”.

This game was a pure joy to make, because the moment I came up with the idea it was just a stream of work and creativity with nothing really getting in the way.

After submitting it I was pleased to see a lot of positive feedback, and lots of great constructive criticism. This feedback holds a ton of stuff that can be learned from.

Let’s start with the good stuff.

LD 42: The good stuff

Takeaway 1: Never forget the juice!

The game was supposed to be expansive, high paced and action packed. I put some considerable polish to the “feel” of the game, and I feel like it paid off. I was super happy to see one comment distinctly mention that “This game has a lot of ‘juice’ “. And you can never have too much juice. The machine gun sound, screen shake, and the 2 separate music tracks were all things that I made to make the game feel good to play and watch. Of course there’s always room for improvement here, (Like I said, can’t have too much juice!) but I was damn pleased with the results there.

Takeaway 2: Never shut down a fun idea, even if it’s late in the jam!

I’d say the second best thing about it was the boss fight at the end of the game. Initially there wasn’t gonna be a boss fight, it was just “shoot enemies, go to planets, escape the galaxy!”. But after making the game for a while, I couldn’t help but want there to be something at the center of it all, at the core of the cataclysm. I had so much fun making the boss. Truth be told, it’s a shame that I made the boss kind of an afterthought and not central to the game, if I knew how much fun I’d have making it from the start, I would have made the entire thing a boss rush. (I would have no problem making the next LD game a boss rush, haven’t seen any of those come out of LD before!)

Enough positivity, let’s get critical!

LD 42: The bad stuff

Takeaway 3: Never bet 100% on ambition, always be ready to scale down!

Right off the bat, this game was overambitious. I wanted to make a game where you were a lone pilot running away from a galactic “gray-goo” invasion, traveling from planet to planet before the goo came down and destroyed it. I had this idea in my mind where you’d land on a planet and you’d have to explore it in haste while an ominous timer ticked down on your HUD. When that timer reached zero, a gray goo pod would crash down like a meteor, spelling doom for the planet. (In my head it was kinda like Dino Run, where there would be this super intense black storm absorbing the entire world and you had to haul ass to run away from it). I clearly didn’t have the time to make something like that, so I decided to go small: Planets kept super simple, with only a handful of enemies and only a few collectables (health and warp drive tanks).

Takeaway 4: Never shy from throwing away stuff that just isn’t fun!

This ambition created a few problems. For instance, I didn’t come up with a good reason to make the players visit any planets. To make that happen, I slapped together a collectable called an “escape tank drive” which I thought would enable the player to escape the goo and beat the game. After making the boss I realized that objective was dumb, so I made it an optional pickup that boosts your health. I spent so much time trying to make and populate the planets, and they ended up being an optional feature because they just weren’t really all that fun.

Takeaway 5: Simple mechanics, complicated mastery, never the other way around!

Another problem kinda tying into that was the fact that while making the game, I added features and mechanics that made sense to me, the developer, but that would probably be difficult for a new player to get comfy with. Shooting with “W”, strafing and “dodging/side shifting” (no idea if I got that idea from Enter the Gungeon or WipEout) all made sense to me when I implemented them, but there was no time for me to make a tutorial or for me to really “teach” the player how to use these mechanics well.

Main Takeaways, summarized:

Takeaway 1: Never forget the juice!

While making the game, make it juicy!

Takeaway 2: Never shut down a fun idea, even if it’s late in the jam!

Brainstorming never stops! You only got 48 hours, spend them working on the good stuff!

Takeaway 3: Never bet 100% on ambition, always be ready to scale down!

Leaving things “up to the air” might work, but don’t gamble it all! Have a backup plan.

Takeaway 4: Never shy from throwing away stuff that just isn’t fun!

Spending an extra hour on something that isn’t fun is one hour you lose working on something that is! Throw it out!

Takeaway 5: Simple mechanics, complicated mastery, never the other way around!

If your game isn’t as playable, don’t add mechanics: Refine them! People will only play your game for a few minutes!

Conclusion

LD42 was a blast, and there definitely was a lot to learn from. Ideas are whizzing through my head as to what LD43’s theme might be (I’m almost willing to bet money that it’ll be “Absorb your Enemies”, but we’ll see).

I gotta get my homework done so that it doesn’t pile up over the weekend. Next post will probably be made right before LD43.

Cheers!
